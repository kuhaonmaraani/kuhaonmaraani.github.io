OLD CAMERAS

Intensified all-sky imagers

The all-sky imagers that were operated by FMI until 2007 used intensified all-sky cameras which have been manufactured by KeoConsultants. Each imager had telecentric and non-vignetting optics, and the field of view of the fish-eye lens was 180 degrees. The filter wheel had seven slots for narrow bandwidth interference filters. In normal operation one filter holder was empty to allow acquiring non-filtered, star images, which were used for geometric calibration. Every station had filters for green, blue, and red line emission.

The faint images were intensified before the final image was acquired by the CCD camera (B&W) and digitised by the frame grabber card of the station computer. This intensification allows shorter exposure times with less expensive CCD cameras, and typically an exposure takes 500ms. A standard imaging sequence included one green line image every 20 sec, and one blue and red line image every 60 sec. Non-filtered images were taken at least once an hour. All images were stored in 8-bit JPG format with 512x512 pixel resolution. The operation of the imager was controlled by the station computer of the all-sky camera station.

Old all-sky mirror cameras

The "historical" all-sky cameras used a downward-looking film camera and a spherical mirror to image the whole sky. There were also three light standards and a small display that were imaged at the same time. There were no filters, and the colours depended on the type of film that is used. The light standards had a known "colour" and could be used in calibrating the results.

Normally the camera took one image per minute, but it was possible to take images every 20 seconds (for campaigns etc.). There were a couple of fixed exposure times; mostly an exposure of two seconds was used and every tenth image was exposed longe

The operation of the imager is started and stopped manually, and after the film is full, one has to develop it before use. The films are the copied to video tapes from which interesting events are digitised for computer use.


FMI mirror camera sample images

The images taken by the historical cameras (used before 1996) contain information about the date and time when the image was taken. Also, three light standards were also imaged. Note that because this camera construction used a spherical mirror the camera blocked the centre of the image. Thus the green light standard could be placed in the middle of the spherical mirror without losing important details from the zenith

The sample images here have been compressed with lossy compressing JPEG-compression. This means that small details in the images are lost but that the compression ratio is larger.

Colour all-sky cameras

In 2007 FMI purchased two color cameras which replaced the intensified CCD cameras at Kevo and Muonio. The images are read in a fast mode which includes binning in one direction. An additional colour conversion is needed to produce full colour 8-bit jpg images. Due to the broad-band colour matrix integrated on the CCD these instruments capture all light within the visible wavelength range, and are thus very sensitive to light pollution.
