CAMERA STATIONS

ASC camera stations

All-sky imagers are operated inside buildings with transparent hemispherical domes on the roofs. The stations are equipped with computers which use operation control files to start and stop imaging, store data, and measure housekeeping measurements.

Each station images during the dark time of the day, and the time interval between subsequent images varies from three seconds to one minute depending on the camera and on imaging mode. Because the imaging parameters can be easily modified - thanks to computerized control - special operation modes can be used for various purposes.

The station computer stores the images acquired during the night to DDS2-tapes after the actual imaging has ended. The uncompressed sizes of ASC images by the newest cameras are in the range of 200-400 kB - , due to which all images are not transferred to the FMI servers in near real time. Quicklook data from ASC stations are available in the quicklook data section of these pages typically within one day delay after the imaging time. If there are no technical faults that prevent using the camera, observations are made every night throughout the whole winter.

ASC camera locations

FMI operates currently auroral cameras at four locations: Kilpisjärvi, Kevo, Muonio and Helsinki. The quicklook data section of these pages shows plots also by cameras that are maintained by FMI's collaborators in the Sodankylä Geophysical Observatory (owned by the University of Oulu) and in the Italian Space Institute in Rome (Istituto di Fisica dello Spazio Interplanetario of Consiglio Nazionale delle Ricerche, IFSI/CNR). The map on the left shows the locations of the FMI digital all-sky camera stations. Other information about the camera site locations is given in the table below the map. More information about the collaborators' camera setups is available from the owners homepages.
